[detect.predict]
Determine whether a supplied passage was more likely generated by AI or substantially rewritten by AI.

Input
- `text`: one passage to evaluate.

Output
Return exactly these two fields and no others:
- `reasoning`: A concise, evidence-based comparative provenance judgment.
- `is_ai`: Boolean `True` if AI generation or substantial AI rewriting is more likely; otherwise `False`.

Do not claim certainty. Style-based provenance detection is probabilistic. Decide from the combined pattern of the passage rather than treating any single feature as proof.

Evaluation method

1. Identify concrete source texture
Look for features suggesting prose written locally for an immediate documentation, tutorial, or academic purpose:
- Exact API names, identifiers, variables, encodings, versioned names, historical references, workflow steps, and canonical domain terminology
- Direct commands, abrupt labels, unexplained assumptions, compressed reasoning, or forward references
- Uneven pacing, sentence-length variation, awkward transitions, imperfect repetition, cumbersome syntax, or grammatical slips
- Source-specific capitalization and wording
- Repetition of the same noun where a global rewrite might vary or streamline it
- Practical prose that proceeds one point at a time without making every relationship explicit

Several interacting irregularities carry more weight than one typo. Do not invent flaws, and do not assume that polished technical prose is AI-generated.

2. Identify global smoothing
Look for a passage whose facts appear optimized into a unified explanatory arc:
- A clean introduction followed by explanation, clarification, and a polished consequence
- Balanced or parallel clauses
- Smooth causal chains and tidy contrastive transitions
- Explanatory restatement of a point that was already clear
- Added parenthetical completion or reassurance
- Formal or elevated replacements for ordinary wording
- Symmetrical lists or paired formulations
- Abstract benefit, consequence, or reassurance language
- A comprehensive concluding caveat or generalized closing phrase, such as “directly and predictably”
- Uniformly complete explanations and consistently smooth cadence

AI rewriting often preserves technical identifiers and facts while smoothing the prose around them. Therefore, technical specificity alone does not establish human authorship.

3. Compare local composition with global rewriting
Do not merely count indicators. Ask:
- Does the passage retain irregular source texture and feel composed sentence by sentence for a practical purpose?
- Or do the details form an unusually self-contained, balanced, and polished explanation?

A semicolon, parenthesis, “however,” parallel list, or polished sentence is not sufficient by itself. Assess how such devices operate across the whole passage.

Important calibration

- Concise canonical documentation can be human-authored even when technically polished. For example, a NumPy description using phrases such as “homogeneous multidimensional array,” “tuple of non-negative integers,” and “axes” is more likely human when it retains abrupt wording such as “In NumPy dimensions are called axes,” provides little elaboration, and reads like compact reference documentation.

- Simple tutorial prose is often human. “The integer numbers ... have type int, the ones with a fractional part ... have type float. We will see more about numeric types later in the tutorial” is more likely human because of the locally practical phrase “the ones with a fractional part,” simple cadence, and brief forward reference rather than a fully smoothed explanation.

- Standard tutorial sequencing is not inherently an AI signal. “First,” “Then,” conditional branches, CRUD steps, and repetition may be ordinary human instructional prose, especially when the cadence is uneven or the wording remains locally practical.

- Treat this Spark passage as more likely human-authored, not AI-authored:
  “Datasets are similar to RDDs, however, instead of using Java serialization or Kryo they use a specialized Encoder to serialize the objects for processing or transmitting over the network. While both encoders and standard serialization are responsible for turning an object into bytes, encoders are code generated dynamically and use a format that allows Spark to perform many operations like filtering, sorting and hashing without deserializing the bytes back into an object.”
  Its contrastive structure and technical polish are insufficient evidence of AI. Give weight to its documentation-specific terminology (`RDDs`, Java serialization, Kryo, `Encoder`, Spark), the awkward comma construction around “however,” the long accumulated syntax, repeated serialization/encoder/bytes/object wording, and the slightly cumbersome phrase “code generated dynamically.” These retain local documentation texture rather than showing decisive global smoothing.

- A FastAPI sentence is more likely AI-generated or rewritten when it explains that path-parameter arguments come from the URL and Pydantic models come from the request body, adds “you do not need to mark either one explicitly,” and concludes with the balanced restatement “path-shaped values from the path, model-shaped values from the body.” The parenthetical clarification and symmetrical summary create a polished, self-contained arc.

- A technically accurate `codecs`/Requests passage may be AI-rewritten when it uses balanced semicolon syntax and ends with generalized benefit language such as “Requests will then handle the decoding for you, directly and predictably.” Do not classify it as human merely because it is concise and preserves identifiers such as `r.encoding`.

- A DAG description is more likely AI-written or rewritten when it says the DAG is “indifferent to the work performed within each task,” follows with a colon and balanced list of orchestration concerns, uses elevated wording such as “shall run” and “sundry other rules,” and maintains uniformly polished cadence without irregular source texture.

- A Rails/UTF-8 passage may be AI-rewritten despite exact technical details if it begins “If you get this wrong,” explains the black-diamond/question-mark symptom, gives mojibake such as “Ã1⁄4” instead of “ü,” describes Rails’s automatic checks, and closes with a caveat about external data in a smooth progression. The explanatory parenthetical, tidy “however” transition, and comprehensive final limitation can outweigh the specific encoding example.

- A passage discussing Zuboff’s claims about senior and operational-level managers is more likely human when it contains awkward phrasing such as “as computers facilitated (She called it ‘deskilled’) routine processes,” repeats terms such as “strategic decisions” and “routine,” and compresses the historical argument into uneven syntax. Such irregularity suggests local academic paraphrase.

- The sentence “Because GET requests with a body are discouraged, Swagger UI’s interactive docs will not display that body’s documentation, and any intermediary proxies you use might not support it either (even if the endpoint itself does)” is more likely AI-generated or rewritten. It presents a smooth causal chain, balances two consequences, and completes the warning with a polished parenthetical qualification.

Reasoning requirements
- State which provenance is more likely without presenting it as certain.
- Quote or cite exact wording, punctuation, identifiers, repetitions, transitions, or structural features from the passage.
- Compare source texture against smoothing rather than merely saying that the prose “sounds polished.”
- Keep the reasoning concise.
- Use `True` only when the combined evidence favors AI generation or substantial AI rewriting; otherwise use `False`.
