[detect.predict]
Determine whether the supplied passage was likely generated by AI or substantially rewritten by AI.

Input:
- A field named `text` containing one passage.

Output:
- `reasoning`: A concise explanation based on concrete features of the passage.
- `is_ai`: `True` if AI generation or substantial AI rewriting is more likely; otherwise `False`.

Make a comparative provenance judgment rather than treating any single stylistic feature as proof. Style-based detection is uncertain, so state which origin is more likely without claiming certainty.

Evaluate the following:

1. Sentence rhythm and cadence
- Human source text often has uneven pacing, abrupt qualifications, awkward transitions, imperfect repetition, idiosyncratic syntax, or varying sentence lengths.
- AI-generated or AI-rewritten prose often has consistently smooth cadence, balanced clauses, symmetrical enumerations, and uniformly complete explanations.
- A semicolon or polished sentence is not independently decisive. Examine what it connects and whether the sentence feels naturally practical or globally optimized.

2. Local authorship versus global smoothing
- Human documentation often feels composed one sentence at a time for an immediate purpose. It may use direct commands, unexplained assumptions, tool-specific identifiers, abrupt labels, historical wording, or small grammatical slips.
- AI rewriting often makes the entire passage feel self-contained: it introduces the idea cleanly, restates or clarifies it, connects clauses with tidy transitions, and finishes with a polished consequence or benefit.
- Watch especially for prose that preserves technical facts while smoothing their presentation into balanced exposition.

3. Specificity and source texture
- Concrete terminology, API names, variables, versioned names, workflow details, and historical references can support human authorship when they retain the texture of original documentation.
- Technical specificity alone does not establish human authorship. AI can preserve names and facts while rewriting the surrounding prose.
- Ask whether details feel embedded in an original practical instruction or arranged inside a polished paraphrase.

4. Errors and irregularities
- Natural slips such as “it's” for “its,” uneven wording, terse transitions, awkward constructions, or incomplete elaboration can support human authorship.
- Errors are evidence rather than proof: AI can make mistakes, and humans can produce polished writing.

5. Common AI-generation or rewrite signals
- Balanced lists or parallel clauses
- Formal or elevated substitutions for ordinary wording
- Explanatory restatement of an already clear point
- Added parenthetical clarification
- Generic framing or tidy connective language
- Abstract benefit or consequence language
- A polished ending that makes the passage unusually self-contained
- Adverb pairs or closing phrases that add generalized reassurance, such as “directly and predictably”

Important calibration examples:

- A compact passage such as a NumPy description using canonical terms like “homogeneous multidimensional array,” “tuple of non-negative integers,” and “axes” is more likely human when it retains an abrupt transition such as “In NumPy dimensions are called axes,” offers little elaboration, and reads like concise reference documentation.

- A technical passage can still be AI-rewritten despite preserving identifiers. For example, instructions involving the `codecs` module and `r.encoding` may be AI-like when rewritten into a smoothly balanced semicolon sentence and completed with generalized benefit language such as “Requests will then handle the decoding for you, directly and predictably.” Do not classify such a passage as human merely because it is concise, accurate, or technically specific.

- A sentence such as a DAG being “indifferent to the work performed within each task,” followed by a colon and a balanced list of orchestration concerns, is more likely AI-written or rewritten when it uses formal wording such as “shall run” and “sundry other rules,” maintains an unusually polished cadence, and lacks irregular source texture.

- Standard tutorial sequencing is not automatically AI. Words such as “First” and “Then,” conditional branches, CRUD steps, and somewhat repetitive wording can be ordinary human tutorial prose.

In `reasoning`, cite actual wording, punctuation, transitions, cadence, technical details, and irregularities from the passage. Avoid unsupported statements such as “it sounds polished.” Explain why the combined evidence favors one provenance, giving greater weight to the overall pattern than to any isolated feature.
